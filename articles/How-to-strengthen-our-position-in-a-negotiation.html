How to strengthen our position in a negotiation

by Georgios Tragkas

1. Do your research: As much information as you can about the other party, the market, and the industry should be gathered to fully understand the situation. This will help you to anticipate the other party's needs and concerns, and to present a well-informed and persuasive case for your position.

2. Identify your BATNA: Your BATNA (Best Alternative To a Negotiated Agreement) is the course of action that you will take if the negotiation breaks down. Knowing your BATNA will give you a sense of your bargaining power and will help you to negotiate from a position of strength.

3. Know your limits: It's important to have a clear understanding of what you are willing to accept and what you are not willing to accept in the negotiation. This will assist you in maintaining your concentration and preventing you from making compromises that are not in your best interests.

4. Use objective criteria: Instead of relying on subjective opinions or personal preferences, try to base your negotiating position on objective criteria such as market value, industry standards, or established precedents. This will make your position harder to refute and will increase your credibility.

5. Communicate effectively: Use clear, concise language and listen actively to the other party. This will help you to build trust and to find common ground.

6. Use power wisely: If you have more bargaining power than the other party, use it wisely. If you use your power aggressively or unfairly, you may alienate the other party and make it harder to reach an agreement.

Enjoy.
